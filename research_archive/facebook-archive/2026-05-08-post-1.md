How the Crazy Hippie Carey Became the Data Scientist Carey.
For most of my life, I was running the wrong program.
Not broken. Not lazy. Not lost.
Just — misconfigured.
I'm an autistic data scientist.
And the thing nobody tells you about being autistic is this: the skill that eventually defines your career is the same one that was quietly exhausting you for decades — long before you understood what it was or what to do with it.
For me, that skill is pattern recognition.
From the time I could speak until well into adulthood, nearly every resource I had was allocated to one thing: fear-based survival.
Every single human interaction was a scientific experiment.
How do I get these people to understand me? How do I communicate with people who seem to operate on a completely different frequency?
That was the literal thought. Every time.
So I mastered the performance. Nice. Polite. Palatable.
I learned what was safe to say, and when I was allowed to open my mouth — always against the backdrop of that historical fear of being the kind of person society wants to silence.
That fear was present in every moment. Every single one.
At some point, for the sake of my own survival, I had to be brutally honest:
Everything I was doing was for everyone else.
So I stopped.
I stopped using the news cycle as a primary source of signals.
I reduced social media to a simple log — ideas posted without caring who responded.
I rebuilt how I sourced information from the ground up.
And I began to prioritize one thing above everything else: improving my own internal processing.
I began to trust methods that survived evidence checks rather than just accepting what I was told.
Continuously. Relentlessly.
That is a mathematical process. That was a conscious decision.
Here's the moment it clicked.
Picture hippie Carey — pulling over at a clover field, jumping out of the car, running through it picking four-leaf clovers. Four-leaf. Another four-leaf. Five-leaf. Six. Seven.
One after another. Without slowing down.
What was actually happening?
I was running pattern recognition at full speed. Scanning. Filtering signal from noise. Identifying anomalies in a dense field of data points.
It was there the whole time.
I just wasn't pointing it at anything with intention.
My friends used to call what I did magic.
Beautiful, mysterious, spiritual magic.
It wasn't supernatural. It was math. The underlying process was visual search, anomaly detection, and signal extraction from noise — operating as an unformalized perceptual system.
Data science gave that process a grammar.
It gave me a way to turn perception into variables, variables into models, models into tests, and tests into decisions.
That is exactly why I am a data scientist. Full stop.
Am I a Da Vinci? No — and that is not the point.
I don't need the comparison. Historical genius is not the unit of analysis here, and retroactively assigning IQ scores to people who lived before psychometrics existed is mostly mythology.
My IQ is 114: high-average, not genius. Useful, but not mythic.
That clarity actually helped me.
It removed the fantasy variable and replaced it with a better question:
What class of problem am I correctly shaped to solve?
Some problems need extreme specialization. Some need institutional authority. Some need the fastest executor. But some problems need a person who can step above the system, see the hidden dependency, and follow the thread down to the foundation.
That is where I become useful.
I don't need recognition. I don't want fame.
I've worked alongside famous people for over twenty years.
I know exactly what that world costs.
I want to get some work done.
Da Vinci isn't why we fly today. His designs aren't the planes in the sky — but he helped. His failures moved the whole project forward.
So is it okay if I fail, please?
I just want a chance to fail. And there is emerging science showing that some forms of deep expertise and abstraction create blind spots under specific conditions — where prior knowledge interferes with finding a better solution. That is exactly where someone with my cognitive profile becomes useful.
Put me above the problem.
I need system-level visibility: the constraints, the data flows, the failure modes, the hidden dependencies. If part of the problem is obscured, my usefulness drops sharply.
I am not optimized for siloed fragments. I am optimized for structure.
Give me the whole system. Let me see the pattern. Then let me follow the thread.
That is how the crazy hippie Carey became the data scientist Carey — not by becoming someone else, but by finally naming the process that had been running the whole time.
The magic was pattern.
The pattern became method.
The method became work.
If you're sitting on a problem that the smartest people in your organization keep circling without solving — I'd genuinely like to hear about it.
Not because I'm certain I can fix it.
Because I'm very good at finding the thread that runs all the way down to the foundation. And I'd like a chance to follow it.
Carey Balboa — Data Scientist
